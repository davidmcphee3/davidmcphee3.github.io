Philosophy of the Mind

Jonathan Haidt outlined three theories of how passion and reason work in our minds in the book The Righteous Mind. I used AI to develop photos of each philosophy by combining the Craiyon tool and the Style Transfer tool of NightCafe.

Copyright Note

All of the following was derived from Jonathan Haidt's book The Righteous Mind. I created a visual representation of certain parts of his book. This is akin to reading a story in school and then making a visual representation paired with an explanation.

The Righteous Mind

The Righteous Mind: Why Good People are Divided by Politics and Religion by Jonathan Haidt is a terrific read and was recommended to me by my younger brother. In Part 1 of this book, Intuitions Come First, Strategic Reasoning Come Second, Haidt outlines three possible theories for how passion and reasoning should interact within our minds. The three theories are from Plato, Thomas Jefferson, and David Hume. Each are explained and then experiments are done to prove which one is correct.

Each of the theories involve two characters: Reason and Passion. Reason is the logic behind decisions and the vocalization of why we do something. Reason is represented by a person. Passion is our intuitions and moral decisions. Passion is our gut instinct and is represented by an elephant.

Plato: Reason is King

Around 400 B.C.E, Plato presented a theory for how passion and reason should interact within our minds. Plato believed that reason should completely control our passions and we should strive to achieve this balance.

Jefferson: Co-Leaders

Thomas Jefferson (3rd U.S President) challenged Plato's theory by arguing that Reason and Passion where actually co-leaders of the mind, ruling over separate domains. Each leader made decisions over certain aspects of our minds but did not consult each other.

Hume: Reason is Slave

David Hume proposed a third explanation, reason is a slave to passions. This theory states that our passions, or gut instincts, come first and reasoning follows. This means that we always retroactively think of reasoning when we make decisions.

AI Creation

To create these images, I used two AI tools: DALL-E mini and Style Transfer. Craiyon hosts the DALL-E mini tool and it works by receiving a text prompt and outputting four images. I started with Craiyon and entered prompts until I got images of what I was looking for. Like any tool, it takes some time to learn what is needed as inputs and how the tool processes prompts. Secondly I took the images and used the Style Transfer tool on NightCafe to make them look painted. Both tools are somewhat free and can be accessed online.
